The Tropic of Calculus I - Derivatives and Integrals

March 27th 2023 - A brief summary of undergraduate calculus.

In this post we will cover the very basics of undergraduate calculus, starting with derivatives and then moving on to integrals, stating some important results upon the way.

Derivatives

For a function $f : \mathbb{R} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$, let $y = f(x)$. We define the derivative to be the function: $$f'(x) = \frac{dy}{dx} := \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} $$ provided the limit exists. It is a function on the values of $x$ for which the above limit exists. From this definition we get the following rules:

  • Product Rule: $$[fg]'(x) = f'(x)g(x) + f(x)g'(x)$$
  • Quotient Rule: $$\left[ \frac{f}{g} \right]'(x) = \frac{g(x)f'(x) - f(x)g'(x)}{g(x)^2}$$
  • Chain Rule: $$ [f(g(x))]' = f'(g(x))g'(x)$$
    • Using these rules we can compute the derivative of many common functions, like polynomials, rational functions and compositions of such functions.

    Here are some useful formulas and results: $$\frac{d}{dx}[x^n] = nx^{n-1},$$ $$\frac{d}{dx}[\sin(x)] = \cos(x), ~~ \frac{d}{dx}[\cos(x)] = -\sin(x),$$ $$e^x := \lim_{n \to \infty}\left(1 + \frac{x}{n} \right)^n, ~~ \frac{d}{dx}[e^{f(x)}] = f'(x)e^x ,$$ $$\ln(e^x) = x = e^{\ln(x)}, ~~ \frac{d}{dx}[\ln(f(x))] = \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)},$$ $$ a^x := e^{a\ln(x)}, ~~ \ln(ab) = \ln(a) + \ln(b), $$ $$ \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\ln(x)}{x^r} = 0 = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^r}{e^x}, ~ \text{ for } r > 0.$$


    The following is a nice example of using the various rules above.

    Example: Compute $y'$ when $y =\ln(x)^{\ln(x)}.$

    If we let $u = \ln(y)$ then we have $u = \ln(x) \cdot \ln(\ln(x)). $ Using the product and chain rule, we find that: $$ u' = \frac{y'}{y} = \frac{1}{x} \ln(\ln(x)) + \frac{1}{x} $$ which results in $y' = \frac{\ln(x)^{\ln(x)}}{x}(\ln(\ln(x)) + 1). $

    Extreme Values

    For $a \in \mathbb{R},$ the value of the derivative $f'(a)$ represents the slope of the tangent line to $f(x)$ at the point $x = a$. Therefore, if $f(x) > 0$ for $x \in [a,b]$ then $f(x)$ is increasing on the interval $[a,b]$. Likewise, $f(x)$ is decreasing on an interval if $f'(x) < 0$ on the interval.

    We can therefore use the derivatives of a function $f(x)$ to determine its local extreme values, i.e. its maximum and minimum values.

  • Maxima/Minima: If $x = c$ is a local maximum (resp. minimum) of a differentiable function $f(x)$, then $f'(c) = 0$.
  • Extreme Value Theorem: If $f(x)$ is differentiable on a closed interval $[a,b]$, then the maximum/minimum value of $f(x)$ occurs at either an endpoint, or at a critical point such that $f'(c) = 0$.
  • Second Derivative Test: Suppose the second derivative $f''(x)$ is continuous around a point $x = c$ and that $f'(c) = 0$. If $f''(c) > 0$ then $f(x)$ has a local minimum at $x = c$, and if $f''(c) < 0$ then $f(x)$ has a local maximum at $x = c$.

    • Example: Without computing numerical values, decide which is the larger of $e^\pi$ or $\pi^e$.

    We wish to decide whether $e^\pi > \pi^e$ is true or not. By taking logarithms and rearranging, we see that it is equivalent to check whether $$ \frac{\ln(e)}{e} > \frac{\ln(\pi)}{\pi} $$ is true or not. To this end, let $f(x) = \frac{\ln(x)}{x}$. Then we can compute: $$ f'(x) = \frac{1 - \ln(x)}{x^2}. $$ From this we can deduce that $f'(e) = 0$ is the unique critical point. Also, $f'(x) > 0 $ for $x < e$ and $f'(x) < 0$ for $x > e$. It therefore follows that $f(x)$ is constantly increasing until $x=e$, whereby it has a global maximum, and then decreases constantly thereafter. It follows that $f(e) > f(\pi)$, so we may conclude that indeed: $$ e^\pi > \pi^e. $$

    Integration

    Integration is a summing process, where we accumulate values of the function over an interval. It requires more setup than the derivative.

    Let $f(x)$ be a function defined on a closed interval $[a,b]$. Subdivide $[a,b]$ into $n$ even subintervals $[a, x_1], \dots, [x_{n-1}, b]$ each with width $\Delta x$. For each subinterval, choose an element $x_i^* \in [x_{i-1}, x_i]$ (a common choice is to take $x_i^*$ to be one of the endpoints). We then define the integral of $f(x)$ between $a$ and $b$ to be: $$ \int_a^b f(x)dx := \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \sum_{i = 1}^n f(x_i^*) \Delta x $$ provided the limit exists. For $f(x)$ piecewise-continuous, the limit will always exist.

    Here are some of the main results regarding integration:

  • Evaluation Theorem: Let $F(x)$ be an antiderivative of $f(x)$ (that is, $F'(x) = f(x)$). Then $$ \int_a^b f(x) dx = F(b) - F(a). $$ For this reason, we adopt the notation that $\int f(x)dx$ is an antiderivative for $f(x)$, and is well defined up to a constant.
  • Fundamental Theorem of Calculus: We have that $$ \frac{d}{dt} \int_a^t f(x)dx = f(t) = \int_a^t f'(x) dx. $$ That is, when defined, integration and differentiation are inverse operators.
  • Substitution: We have that $$ \int f'(g(x))g'(x)dx = \int f(u)du $$ where $u = g(x)$. This is the integral inversion of the chain rule.
  • Integration By Parts: We have that $$ \int u dv = uv - \int v du. $$ This is derived from the product rule for differentiation, and is used mainly when the integral on the right is simpler than the one on the left.

    • Example: Compute the integral $\int_{0}^{\infty} e^{-x^2/2}dx$.

    Let $I = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2/2}dx$ be the value we wish to compute. Then $$ I^2 = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2/2}dx \cdot \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-y^2/2}dy $$ and so combining the integrals (this requires Fubini's Theorem) we obtain: $$ I^2 = \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-(x^2 + y^2)/2}dxdy. $$ We now make the substitutions $x = r\cos(\theta)$ and $y = r \sin(\theta)$ (these are called polar coordinates) and the integral becomes: $$ I^2 = \int_0^\infty \int_{0}^{2\pi} e^{-r^2/2} rdrd\theta = \left[ e^{-r^2/2} \right]^\infty_0 \cdot \left[\theta \right]^{ 2\pi}_0 = 2\pi.$$ Thus we have that: $$ \int_0^\infty e^{-x^2/2}dx = \frac{1}{2}I = \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2}}.$$ This example actually shows that the function $f(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \pi}} e^{-x^2/2}$ integrates to 1, and thus defines a valid probability density function (pdf) on the interval $(0,\infty)$. This is none other than the standard normal distribution.

    One of the main applications of integration in undergraduate calculus is to calculate areas and volumes.

    Volumes of Solids

    The 3D volume of a surface is given by $$ \int_{a}^{b} A(z)dz $$ where $A(z)$ is the cross-sectional area obtained by intersecting the surface with a plane that is perpendicular to the $z$-axis.

    Example: The snow began to fall at some point in time before noon, at a constant rate. The city sent out a snowplow at noon to clear the snow. The plow removed snow at a constant volume per minute. By $1$pm, the plow had moved $2$ miles. By $2$pm, it had moved $3$ miles. When did the snow begin to fall?

    Let noon be $t = 0$ and suppose snow began to fall $T$ hours before noon. The more snow there is, the slower the plow will travel. Therefore, if $v$ is the slowplow's velocity, we have $$ v = \frac{c_1}{A(t)} $$ where $c_1$ is some constant, and $A(t)$ is the cross-sectional area of the snow. If $t$ represents time in the afternoon, then $A(t) = c_2(t+T)$ where $c_2$ is the constant rate of snowfall per hour. Thus we have that $v = \frac{c}{t+T}$ for some constant $c$. Integrating to equate the volumes we have: $$ \int_0^1 \frac{c}{t+T}dt = c\ln \left(\frac{1+T}{T} \right) = 2 $$ $$ \int_0^2 \frac{c}{t+T}dt = c\ln \left(\frac{2+T}{T} \right) = 3. $$ Equating we find that $$ T^2 - T + 1 = 0 ~~\Longrightarrow~ T = \frac{\sqrt{5}-1}{2}.$$
